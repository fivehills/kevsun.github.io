About Me

I am a full professor of computational linguistics and cognitive science at the College of Foreign Languages, Tongji University, China. I have completed my Habilitation (professor qualification) and previously worked as a research scientist at the University of Tübingen, Germany. Before that, I held associate professor positions at two Chinese universities.

My research spans computational linguistics, deep learning, machine learning, and computational cognition. I have conducted extensive research in these areas using both computational and experimental methodologies. My work has been widely recognized in the academic community, with publications in reputable journals and successful competitive research grant applications.

For more detailed information about my academic background, research projects, and publications, please refer to my CV or explore the sections below.

Research Interests

Large Language Models (LLMs)

My primary research focus is on fine-tuning and optimizing Large Language Models (LLMs), evaluating their performance, and enhancing their reasoning capabilities. Additionally, I specialize in developing AI-driven multi-agent systems and industry-specific LLM applications, including chatbots, text generation, data processing, information retrieval, and database management.

Drawing on years of industry experience as a freelance professional, I have refined techniques such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), mixed prompting strategies, and evaluation frameworks for complex tasks.

Computational Linguistics

In computational linguistics, I am actively developing probabilistic multilingual discourse parsers using LLMs and constructing diverse linguistic databases and corpora. I also leverage open-source LLMs to build applications for detecting human emotion and cognition from multimodal data, with applications in personality assessment, mental health detection, and other psychological domains.

Computational Cognition

My work focuses on convolution-based computational frameworks to model and predict human language comprehension, language production, and multimodal processing using eye-tracking, EEG, and fMRI data. I am also pioneering the integration of automatic linguistic analysis with neurocognitive research.

Additionally, I investigate syntax, pragmatics, and discourse using both computational and experimental approaches, with the overarching goal of deepening our understanding of how humans and machines process language.

Teaching

Advanced Computational Linguistics (Graduate)
This course covers advanced topics in computational linguistics, including probabilistic language models, discourse parsing, and applications of deep learning in natural language processing. Students engage in hands-on projects involving implementation of computational models.
Large Language Models: Theory and Applications (Graduate)
This course introduces the architecture, training, and fine-tuning of large language models. Topics include transformer architectures, prompt engineering, retrieval-augmented generation, and ethical considerations in AI deployment.
Cognitive Models of Language Processing (Graduate)
This interdisciplinary course examines computational models of human language processing, drawing on insights from linguistics, psychology, and neuroscience. Students learn to design experiments and analyze data from eye-tracking, EEG, and fMRI studies.
Introduction to Natural Language Processing (Undergraduate)
This course provides a comprehensive introduction to NLP, covering text processing, language modeling, syntax parsing, and semantic analysis. Students develop practical skills in Python programming for text analysis.
Corpus Linguistics (Undergraduate)
This course introduces students to corpus-based approaches to linguistic analysis. Topics include corpus design, annotation standards, statistical methods for corpus analysis, and applications in lexicography and language teaching.
